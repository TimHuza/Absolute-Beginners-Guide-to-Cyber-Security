### Social Engineering in Cybersecurity

Now, going back to the cybersecurity part of social engineering.

It is more dangerous than traditional methods of hacking as it relies on human error, which is subjective and less predictable than software or hardware vulnerabilities.

So here's the thing, okay.

If you're a security analyst or you're in charge of the security of your company, when you're working with software and hardware you know what you're getting, right?

Software is predictable, hardware is predictable.

You know, if you're getting the best firewalls, the best anti malware, you know you're gonna get the best results.

But when you're dealing with human beings, human beings are not software, we're not hardware.

We might be predictable in certain aspects of our life but in general, we are nowhere near as predictable as a software or a hardware.

And this is the most dangerous aspect of social engineering because it doesn't matter how much training employees get, social engineering, especially if it's being conducted by a very good social engineer, there is still a high chance or high probability that they might end up falling victims.

So it is a very, very, very dangerous form of attack.

### Main Tactics of Social Engineering

Now, there are five main tactics associated with social engineering:

- Baiting  
- Pretexting  
- Quid pro quo  
- Phishing  
- Vishing  

---

### Baiting

Trying to lure the target to taking an unfavorable action.

The social engineer tries to bait the victim.

Examples:
- Use of links in emails (phishing)
- Dropping infected USB drives in strategic locations

Imagine you're walking out of your office and you're in the car park, and then you see a USB drive on the floor.

What would you do?

There is a very good chance you might become curious.

You pick it up, plug it into your computer, and end up infecting your entire network.

This has happened in real life.

So that is baiting — it relies on curiosity.

---

### Pretexting

Very, very powerful.

Looking, acting, or sounding the part.

Relies on conviction and trust.

Example:

Someone wearing a telecommunications company shirt (e.g., AT&T) approaches you.

You might automatically trust them just based on appearance.

That’s pretexting — creating a believable identity.

---

### Quid Pro Quo

Offering a service in exchange for information.

Example:

A contractor working inside a company casually asks employees “innocent” questions like:
- How often do you change passwords?
- What are your security practices?

A skilled social engineer knows how and when to ask these questions so the victim doesn’t realize they're giving away sensitive information.

---

### Kevin Mitnick

Kevin Mitnick is considered one of the greatest social engineers in cybersecurity.

He gained access to highly sensitive data primarily through social engineering rather than technical hacking.

He is widely regarded as one of the greatest hackers of all time.

---

### Phishing & Vishing

- **Phishing**: Uses emails to create urgency, fear, or excitement  
- **Vishing**: Uses phone calls instead of emails  

Both will be discussed further in the next videos.

---

### Real-World Example (Vishing)

A well-known example involves a social engineer calling a telecom support desk.

She pretends to be in distress (baby crying in background, emotional story).

She convinces the support agent to:
- Grant access to a customer account
- Change the password
- Reveal sensitive personal data

This demonstrates how human emotion can override security training.

---

### Final Advice

If you work in customer support or any role involving communication:

- Be extremely cautious  
- Do not rely solely on intuition or sympathy  
- Follow security protocols strictly  

Social engineers are highly skilled and can manipulate even trained individuals.
