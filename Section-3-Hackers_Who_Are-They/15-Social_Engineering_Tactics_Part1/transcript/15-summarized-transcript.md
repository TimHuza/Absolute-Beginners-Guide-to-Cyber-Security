# Social Engineering — Summary

## What is Social Engineering?
- The exploitation of human emotions and interactions to extract sensitive information  
- Not limited to cybersecurity — exists in negotiations, scams, and everyday interactions  

## Why It’s Dangerous
- Relies on human behavior, which is unpredictable  
- More effective than traditional hacking (software/hardware attacks)  
- Even trained individuals can fall victim  

## Real-World Example
- *Catch Me If You Can* (Frank Abagnale Jr.)
  - Successfully impersonated multiple professionals
  - Demonstrates the power of manipulation and trust  

## Main Social Engineering Tactics

### 1. Baiting
- Luring victims into harmful actions  
- Example: infected USB drives, malicious links  
- Exploits curiosity  

### 2. Pretexting
- Creating a believable identity to gain trust  
- Example: impersonating a company employee  

### 3. Quid Pro Quo
- Offering something in exchange for information  
- Example: asking “innocent” questions while working inside a company  

### 4. Phishing
- Using emails to create urgency or fear  
- Tricks users into revealing information  

### 5. Vishing
- Using phone calls instead of emails  
- Often involves emotional manipulation  

## Notable Figure
- **Kevin Mitnick**
  - Famous social engineer and hacker  
  - Gained access to sensitive data through manipulation rather than technical exploits  

## Key Takeaway
- Humans are the weakest link in security  
- Social engineering succeeds by exploiting trust, emotion, and behavior  
- Awareness and strict adherence to security protocols are critical
