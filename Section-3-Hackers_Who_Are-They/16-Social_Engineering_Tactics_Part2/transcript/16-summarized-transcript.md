# Section 3: Hackers - Who Are They

## 16. Social Engineering Tactics - Part 2 (Summary)

### Overview
- Focus on common workplace social engineering techniques.
- Emphasizes how human behavior is often the weakest security link.

---

### Lunch Time Attack
- Occurs when employees leave workstations without logging out.
- Insider threats can access sensitive information during this time.
- **Prevention:** Always lock or log out of your workstation.

---

### Piggybacking
- Attacker gains access by exploiting human kindness (e.g., holding doors).
- Often involves carrying items or creating believable scenarios.
- Relies on social pressure and trust.

---

### Tailgating
- Attacker closely follows an authorized person into a restricted area.
- Unlike piggybacking, no direct interaction or permission is required.
- Often involves acting confident and blending in.

---

### Shoulder Surfing
- Attacker observes someone entering sensitive information (e.g., passwords).
- Less common but still a risk, especially in public spaces like ATMs.

---

### Dumpster Diving
- Retrieving sensitive information from discarded materials.
- Common due to improper disposal of documents or devices.
- **Prevention:** Properly shred documents and destroy storage media.

---

### Key Takeaway
- Technical security measures are ineffective if human behavior is exploited.
- Social engineering is powerful because it targets trust and routine actions.

---

### Prevention Strategies
- Train employees to recognize social engineering tactics.
- Stay observant of unfamiliar individuals without proper access.
- Ask questions and verify identities.
- Report suspicious behavior to security.
- Properly dispose of sensitive information (shredding, destroying devices).

---

### Final Thought
- Even strong security systems can fail if people unknowingly allow access.
- Awareness and cautious behavior are critical for protection.
